Scandinavian rhotics

Standard varieties of both Norwegian and Swedish have flap [ɾ]; backed phonetic realisations are associated with specific dialect regions.

  • norwegian: uvular [ʁ] is characteristic of southwest Norway, and diffusion outward from Bergen and Kristiansand took place slowly & is still ongoing (Trudgill 1973, Trudgill & Chambers 1998).
  • swedish: uvular [ʁ] is characteristic of the dialects of southern Sweden (Skåne, Blekinge, Halland, Småland); understood to have spread northward during the 20th century (Sjöstedt 1936, Elert 1994), although now unclear if still ongoing.

In both cases: variation in the relationship of uvular rhotics to phonological rules targeting rhotics (typically pattern with alveolar rhotics in Norwegian retroflexion, typically block Swedish retroflexion).

Scandinavian rhotics

Both Norwegian and Swedish uvular [ʁ] have been associated with 'Danish'.

  • Trudgill 1974: uvular rhotics have really spread across western and northern Europe from French-speaking regions in a hierarchical manner (that is, hopping from one large urban centre to another): in this picture, the uvular rhotic arrives in Copenhagen as a precondition for its spread in the more distant Scandinavian areas.
  • Danish was the standard language in modern Norway until c19; 'Southern Swedish' = Danish territory pre–1658.

    Chambers & Trudgill 1998 (11): Scandinavian dialect continuum.

Scandinavian rhotics

Danish itself:

  • Relatively poorly described (for a Northern European language!). As such, we don't know the historical/geographical/phonological distribution of rhotic realisations in Danish, other than a few claims about the standard and the conjecture (above) that (some varieties of) Danish must have been prior to Norwegian and Swedish.
  • Historically: how far did the uvular rhotic get across Danish varieties?
  • Descriptively and phonetically: what segments are we really dealing with?

Danish

Grønnum 1997 (illustration of the IPA): only makes claims about the Copenhagen standard, but there gives [ʁ] (or devoiced χ). Similar description in Basbøll 2005: [ʁ~ʁ̞] and, citing Grønnum 1998 (pedagogical material), voiceless [χ]. But Ladefoged & Maddieson (1996: 323): [ʕ̞], Ladefoged & Johnson (2011/6th ed.): pharyngeal approximant.

General complications: (historical?) rhotics and other C are much more frequently deleted & lenited than in other Scandinavian languages, further interactions between (intervocalic/coda) rhotic deletions and final C glottalisation & pharyngealisation. The Copenhagen suburb of Amager is [ˈɑmɑːˀ], and an Amager resident (an amagerer) is [ˈɑmɑːˀɑ]. Seems to have been prior to the spread of new rhotic realisations; 'best' tokens are often initial Cr clusters.

The varieties of Jutland

The varieties of Jutland

A bit of motivation for considering Jutland:

  • The largest contiguous region in Denmark, so a good test case for ideas about the diffusion of the uvular rhotic;
  • Relatively rural and conservative. Basbøll 2005 (218): Danish apical trill 'could be heard until recently in very old forms of certain conservative dialects in Northern Jutland and Bornholm'.
  • No phonetic analysis of any phenomenon in any Jutland variety exists, which seems like a large gap.

Examples that follow: recordings from the archives of the Center for Dialect Research @ Copenhagen University); collected for traditional dialectological purposes, so very conservative speech of non-mobile rural older males.

Segmentation & analysis

Segmentation at the onset of visible frication noise or formant structure, categorised by DG and MH auditorily (which 77% agreement) & on inspection of the signal.

  • Total duration (ms)
  • Percentage of duration for which glottal pulsing is present (i. e. fraction voicing)
  • Mean intensity (dB)
  • First spectral moment (centroid), where possible, for the middle 80% of the duration of the segment.

A story

  • One vast oversimplification is that an approximant realisation and a fricative realisation are competing, diagnosed primarily by susceptibility to voicing-assimilation –
  • – and center-periphery geography around the large city of Aarhus suggests that the approximant realisation is the more recent introduction into the system.

Approximants in Jutland

  • Approximant realisations of the rhotic are spreading to new contexts – but they keep their very posterior place of articulation, & their surface- level frication.
  • Approximant realisations of the rhotic are spreading both through the population and to new phonological contexts – but they keep their very posterior place of articulation, & their surface- level frication.
  • This spread sets up a contrast: between systems in which the fortis-lenis contrast – whose phonetic reflex is VOT – in stops is always reflected in a following rhotic, & systems that sometimes lose it.
  • What are the implications for the status of the rhotic? A very structuralist claim here is that there's some kind of real representational change (blocking 'assimilation' to the phonologically-voiceless series of stops), but where should we go for counter-evidence?
