About the project

  • ESRC project ‘Investigating the diffusion of morphosyntactic innovations using social media’, September 2017—July 2021.
    • David Willis (PI, Oxford)
    • Adrian Leemann (CI, Bern)
    • Tam Blaxter (CI, Cambridge)
    • Deepthi Gopal (RA, Cambridge)
    • Jonathan Mackenzie (IT, Cambridge).
  • Geospatial patterns of morphosyntactic variation in Twitter corpora for the English of UK and Ireland, Welsh, Norwegian/mainland Scandinavian; also Turkish, Icelandic, Slovene, Haitian Creole.
    • Much, although not all existing work using large-scale social-media data: frequent, often lexical patterns; very large languages, like American English; very large geographical scales (North America, 'the world').(Eisenstein et al. 2014; Russ 2012; Doyle 2014; Bamman et al. 2014; Gonçalves & Sánchez 2014; Grieve et al. 2013; Grieve et al. 2017; Grieve et al. 2018; Grieve et al. 2019)
    • Our general aim: smaller languages, finely-grained geographical patterning, extension to apparent time.
    • Focus for today's talk:
      • patterns in the UK and Ireland corpus, and filling in geographical gaps in our understanding of recent innovations.

The corpus

  • The GB/IE English corpus in all: 6,121,716 unique users responsible for 546,398,943 tweets.
    • Tweets have two sources: one is the Twitter 'streaming' API, which allows us to download all tweets Twitter-geotagged within a geographical bounding box around the UK and Ireland. This kind of geotagging is opt-in, so biased; we also downloaded data corresponding to posts by users mentioned by the users in the streaming data, accounting for some representation of Tweets posted before the start of data collection.

Inferred metadata


Figure: Localised users over a 10km grid.
  • 1,963,409 users were classified for location and determined to come from the UK, Ireland or the Isle of Man: each user is associated with a latitude-longitude pair corresponding to an individual lowest-level place in the ONS 'Index of Place Names' (England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland) or a townland in the Ordnance Survey of Ireland (Republic of Ireland).
    • mentions of low-level places (‘Cambridge’, ‘Ballymaguigan’) and regions, both formally- and informally-defined (‘Herefordshire’; ‘the North’; ‘Norn Iron’), weighted by number of occurrences, context, and distance between the places mentioned, in user location field, bio, or tweet text
    • interaction with a set of ‘official’ accounts manually determined to correspond to specific local areas (local government and services — police, fire, county and city councils, health authorities, and school boards).

Inferred metadata

  • 294,121 users were classified for age, at different levels of granularity:
    • precise classifications based on exact matches for age or birth year ('I was born in ...', 'It's my ___th birthday today')
    • fine age categories ('I'm in my 40s', 'I'm a 90s kid')
    • broad age categories (scored based on a dictionary of keywords — ‘retired’, ‘dad of three’, ’college student’, ‘Fortnite’ ...).
  • Differences in the distribution of informativeness across different age groups.
    • Users who specified actual ages or birthyears were disproportionately the youngest users; users binned into the middle category were disproportionately those who gave keywords not associated with the youngest group (generally occupational or familial), but no further information with which to narrow down.

    Figure. Left: number of users in each broad age category, sub-categorised by decade-level ‘fine’ category. Right: users for whom birthyear-level age was inferred.

Today's focus

  • How well does this corpus work? What can we do?
    • Can we deepen or improve upon existing descriptions of the geographical distribution of variation?
  • Our thoughts for today:
    • Twitter data allows us to begin to understand varieties that are relatively under-represented in the sociolinguistic literature, eg. West Midlands.
    • Certain features may characterise a generalised ‘urban British English‘, rather than a more geographically-bounded variety like Multicultural London English. For this (short!) talk:
      • Preposition deletion;
      • bare as intensifier and quantifier.

Preposition drop

  • The presence of constructions with no overt preposition where ‘standard’ varieties of English would have ’to’ has been reported for several different varieties of English.
    • Lancashire. Today I’m going the library. (Myler 2011, 2013; Biggs 2014)
    Liverpool. Swim the end and back. (Biggs 2014)
    Manchester. She went the pub. (Haddican 2010)
    London, Kent. I haven't been Shoreditch in ages. (Bailey 2018, Hall 2019)
  • These different dialects seem to correspond to syntactic differences.
    • In the south-east (Bailey 2018, Hall 2019):
      • the determiner is absent (go pub, but not go the pub);
      • the verb must be directional go or come;
      • modification (straight the pub, right the pub) is impossible.
    • In the north-west:
      • the determiner is present: I'm going the pub (Haddican 2010, John came the pub with me (Biggs 2014);
      • a wider range of verbs (come, go, run, drive, nip, jog) is available in Lancashire (Myler 2011), and wider still in Liverpool (Joe plodded the pub; swim the end and back; Biggs 2014).
      • modification (straight the pub, right the pub) is impossible in Lancashire (Myler 2011) but possible in Liverpool (Biggs 2014).

Preposition drop

  • This rather disjoint picture of the overall geographical distribution of preposition deletion in British English corresponds both to descriptive gaps in the literature, and to variation in the extent to which this feature is considered regionally or sociolinguistically particular:
      • Bare NPs after GO are heard in young people’s informal English, whether or not they are speakers of the multiethnolect, but they are far more frequent in the multiethnolect [Multicultural London English]. (Cheshire, Nortier & Adger 2015: 11)
    • Described as a feature of the speech of “a subset of English speakers in London (and some surrounding areas)” by Hall (2019), who treats the London pattern as a different phenomenon to that reported for varieties in the UK;
    • Bailey (2016, 2018): preposition drop occurs in ‘southeast English’, noting that this is deliberately vague and the boundaries are unknown.
  • Should we take the existing data to reflect a high degree of interspeaker variation within preposition-drop varieties, or more systematic regional variation?

Preposition drop: our data


Figure: Preposition drop across all contexts and all users.
  • We searched the corpus for all forms of the verbs go, come, walk, head, return, drive, travel, fly, rush, swim, returning 761,361 posts by 505,580 unique users. Of these, we could associate location metadata with 597,945 tweets posted by 306,100 unique users, and age metadata with 178,604 posts by 45,307 users.
  • What we seem to be able to add to the existing literature is the missing data about geographically-intermediate regions: across all contexts, preposition drop is found at the highest rates in our data in the West Midlands, with a clear, spatially-contiguous region running from Liverpool to London distinguished from the surrounding area by high rates of preposition deletion.
    • A secondary zone of innovation in the South-West.

bare as quantifier and intensifier

  • bare with various functions is listed as a ‘core characteristic’ feature of ‘Multicultural London English’ by Kircher & Fox (2019:849), citing Kerswill (2013).
    • Núñez-Pertejo & Palacios-Martínez (2018) find that its use peaks among younger teenagers; most frequently as a quantifier (“I ... got bare friends”, “you just see bare people”, “I can hear bare noise”), especially with ‘people’; sometimes nominalised as bares (“I failed bares”, “end up with money ... bares of it”); also as an adjectival intensifier (“bare good looking”, “bare mean”); and sometimes in other functions (“he was bare like ‘...’”, “I got bare out of maths”).
      • Not as well-studied a feature, but if there is a previous proposal here for the geospatial distribution of the various functions of bare, it is a proposal for a distribution centred around London.

    Figure. Left: bare as quantifier. Right: bare as intensifier.

Further thoughts

  • Hopefully we've demonstrated that this corpus works — and allows us to fill in gaps in the existing data.
  • We don't envisage that this kind of study replaces traditional sociolinguistic studies, but it has strengths in allowing us to get the kind of large-scale geographical picture that it's difficult to achieve with interview data.
  • We think it's possible that these data (and more) support the proposals of Drummond (2017), among others — that many innovative features are shared between urban centres across
  • And it seems to be possible to infer something about apparent time from Twitter data!
